A Day in the Wild Jungle Safari at Badreni

March 10, 2026 Badreni Team Safari & Wildlife

There is something profoundly humbling about stepping into the heart of Chitwan's ancient forests at dawn. The mist hangs low over the Rapti River, birds begin their morning chorus, and somewhere in the distance, a onehorned rhinoceros grazes quietly in the tall elephant grass. This is not a nature documentary this is a morning at Badreni Jungle Resort.

Dawn Departure

Our day began at 5:30 AM. The air was crisp and still, carrying the earthy scent of the jungle floor. Our expert naturalist, Ram Bahadur, briefed our small group on what to expect — and more importantly, how to behave in the presence of wild animals. "Walk softly, speak less, observe more," he said with a calm confidence that immediately put us at ease.

We set off on foot through the buffer zone of Chitwan National Park. Within the first fifteen minutes, a spotted deer darted across our path, unaware of our presence. Ram pointed out fresh tracks in the mud — a tiger had passed through just hours before we arrived.

Into the Wild

The forest deepened as we moved away from the resort grounds. Towering Sal trees filtered the early sunlight into golden shafts that illuminated the undergrowth. We spotted a kalij pheasant foraging among fallen leaves, wild boar snuffling near a termite mound, and a magnificent peacock fanning its plumage in a small clearing.

Ram quietly pointed to a treetop where a crested serpent eagle perched motionlessly, its crest raised, scanning the forest floor below. In two hours of walking, we had already encountered more wildlife than most visitors see in an entire day at a crowded reserve.

Sal forest in early morning light

The Rhino Encounter

The highlight of the morning came unexpectedly, as the best wildlife moments always do. We had paused beside a dry riverbed when Ram raised his hand — a silent signal to stop. Twenty metres ahead, half hidden by a curtain of tall grass, stood a Greater One-Horned Rhinoceros. It raised its head, nostrils flaring, then lowered it again to continue grazing.

We stood absolutely still for nearly ten minutes, barely breathing. The rhino was entirely unconcerned by our presence, a testament to years of dedicated conservation efforts in Chitwan National Park. It was a moment none of us will forget.

River Canoeing on the Rapti

After a hearty jungle brunch at the resort, the afternoon called for a different kind of adventure. We boarded traditional dugout canoes on the Rapti River, drifting silently along the bank while our boatman scanned the muddy shores for crocodiles.

Gharial crocodiles lay motionless on sandy banks, warming in the afternoon sun. Kingfishers darted from branch to branch. Smooth-coated otters played in the shallows. The experience was meditative — the rhythmic dip of the paddle, the soft gurgle of water, the silence of the forest around us.

Evening Cultural Performance

As the sun dipped below the treeline, we gathered at the open-air courtyard for a traditional Tharu cultural performance. The Tharu community performed their iconic stick dance and folk songs that have been passed down through generations.

The rhythmic drumming, the vibrant costumes, and the warmth of the community left us feeling deeply connected to this land. It was the perfect close to an extraordinary day — a reminder that wild places still exist, and that we have a responsibility to protect them.

The jungle does not show itself all at once It reveals itself slowly patiently to those who are willing to walk quietly and pay attention
— Ram Bahadur, Lead Naturalist, Badreni Jungle Resort
